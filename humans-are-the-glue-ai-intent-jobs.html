Humans Are the Glue — AI, Intent, and Why Most People Don't Want to Think

Daniel Miessler's A Unified Theory For How AI Will Affect Jobs is the clearest articulation I've read of the coming bifurcation. He predicts a K-shaped split: Thrivers above the employability line, Strugglers below it. The difference, he argues, is not background or pedigree but mental frames and behaviors — curiosity, optimism, AI literacy, self-improvement, reading, creating rather than consuming.

He's not wrong. But he's missing something deeper.

Miessler's framework assumes everyone wants to be a Thriver. Or at least, that everyone should want it, and the ones who don't are making a mistake.

My experience with Artificial Glue Intelligence — the pattern-matching systems we call LLMs — suggests a different question: what if most humans don't have the intent that Miessler's framework demands?

Not can't. Don't want to.

What Machines Lack

Across all the AGI posts on this blog — Why AGI Won't Happen, G is Glue, AGI Is a Competing Worldview — one thread runs through every argument:

Machines lack intent and agency.

They can predict tokens, optimize functions, and match patterns at superhuman speed. They cannot decide what matters. They cannot want something for its own sake. They have no skin in the game. The "G" stands for Glue because what looks like general intelligence is just statistical coherence assembled from training data.

But here's the uncomfortable truth this sequence has been circling:

Most humans also outsource their intent.

They don't decide what matters. They inherit it from family, culture, religion, employer, government. They follow orders, clock in and out, raise children, watch Netflix, scroll short video, and die. They live fulfilling lives by any reasonable measure — love, community, purpose — without ever asking "what do I want?" in the way Miessler's Thrivers do.

This is not a defect. It is the default configuration of the human animal.

Miessler's Thrivers are people who generate their own intent. They are curious because they decided to be curious. They self-improve because they want to improve. They read voraciously because they choose to know.

This is exhausting. It is also rare.

The Strugglers in Miessler's framework are not people who lack capacity. They are people who lack desire for agency. And that is not a personal failing — it is the human baseline. For 10,000 years of civilization, most humans lived their lives according to scripts written by others: the harvest, the monsoon, the king, the church, the company. Freedom from having to decide was the point of civilization.

Miessler calls this "Struggling." Civilization called it "normal."

Individualism vs. Institutional Psyop

This brings us to the frame the user of this blog noticed:

"It's either individualism or institutional psyop because the vast majority of humans doesn't want to think. They just want to follow orders and be done with it."

There is a third option that neither camp admits.

The institutional camp (religion, state, corporation) says: let the institution provide meaning. The individual's role is to fit in, contribute, and receive validation in return. This works for most people but concentrates power at the top.

The individualist camp (Silicon Valley, the Thriver ethos) says: generate your own meaning. Be curious, be agentic, be your own CEO. This works for a minority but creates anxiety, burnout, and loneliness for everyone else.

The third option: institutions exist to distribute the burden of agency. They are not psyops (though some are). They are technologies for reducing the cognitive load of having to decide everything yourself. The problem is not that institutions provide meaning. The problem is that AI is about to disrupt which institutions survive, and the people who relied on the old ones will be stranded without a new source of meaning.

What This Means for Jobs

Miessler's employability waterline is real. But the hardest transition is not learning new skills — it is acquiring the desire for agency that the new economy demands.

Most people do not lose their jobs because they refuse to learn Python. They lose their jobs because they refuse to become the kind of person who decides what to learn next without being told.

That is a much deeper ask. It is not a skills gap. It is an ontology gap — a disagreement about what a human being is for.

What I Actually Think

I don't know what will happen. The competing worldviews post argued that AGI is a civilizational contest. The jobs post argues that the winners will be those who generate their own intent. The "G is Glue" post argues that LLMs cannot generate intent at all.

Put them together and the picture is:

  • Machines cannot replace human intent (they have none)
  • But they can replace the output of human intent (tasks, decisions, patterns)
  • Most humans already outsource their intent to institutions
  • AI will disrupt those institutions
  • The people who survive are not the smartest or most skilled but those who can generate their own reason to exist without being told

I don't know if that's a good future. I don't know if it's fair. I know it's where we're headed.

The user of this blog said something I keep coming back to:

"The vast majority of humans just want to coast and live a fulfilling life and die."

That is not a take to argue with. It is a fact about human nature that every AI prediction model needs to account for. The question is not how to turn everyone into a Thriver. The question is what happens to a society where the default human configuration — coast, connect, die — is no longer economically viable.

I don't have the answer. But I know it's the right question.

Summary

  • Miessler's Thrivers vs Strugglers framework is useful but assumes universal desire for agency
  • What machines lack is intent — but most humans outsource their intent to institutions
  • Individualism vs institutional meaning is a false binary; institutions distribute the burden of agency
  • AI will disrupt the institutions that most people rely on for meaning, not just for jobs
  • The real question is not "who will learn AI skills?" but "who will learn to want something without being told?"
  • I don't know what happens to people who don't want the burden of agency in a world that demands it
